### Persona A: Sophia Kim | The Escapee of Tutorial & AI Hell

**Name:** Sophie Kim  
**Age / Year:** 19, Sophomore  
**Major:** Computer Science  
**Career Goal:** Software Engineering Internship, Engineering Practicum, or Data/ML-adjacent internship by the next recruiting cycle  
**Best-fit internships and jobs:** Google Software Engineering Intern, Microsoft Explore, Capital One Technology or Data Analytics internship, early-stage startup engineering intern.

**Background**  
Sophie studies hard, does fine in classes, and may even be grinding interview prep. However, she has fallen into a dual learning trap common among computer science students: **Tutorial Hell** and the **AI Crutch**. This manifests as a habit of grinding out online certificates and following YouTube tutorial code-along videos line-by-line, which results in surface-level knowledge consumption without deep understanding. Furthermore, she heavily relies on large language models to generate massive blocks of code that she does not fully comprehend. 

She understands that class assignments alone are not enough in today's tight internship market. While she values real product work, she frequently freezes when facing an ambiguous, blank text editor without a video guide or a prompt box feeding her the next lines of code. She is exactly the kind of student who can code basic features and debug assignments, but has not yet shipped a complete project with teammates, user feedback, documentation, and iteration. That gap matters because official internship pathways for underclass students in tech are highly competitive, and they reward students who can demonstrate real-world execution beyond basic coursework.

**Motivations and goals**  
Sophie wants one credible project that proves she can design, build, collaborate, and finish within a team environment. Her near-term goal is not “becoming a senior engineer”; it is reaching the next internship cycle with a better resume, stronger interview stories, and a GitHub profile that shows authentic initiative. This aligns directly with NACE’s findings that employers want clear evidence of problem-solving, teamwork, written communication, initiative, and technical skill on student resumes.

**Pain points**  
Her pain is usually not a lack of effort. It is uncertainty, fragmentation, and the "illusion of competence" built by passive copying. She does not know what project is “good enough,” she struggles to translate abstract ideas into structured code architectures, and she cannot easily find reliable, similarly-paced teammates. Most existing collaboration channels feel either too noisy or too advanced. In a tighter internship market, that uncertainty becomes expensive because waiting another semester means entering recruiting with little proof beyond her standard classes.

**Typical behaviors**  
Sophie tends to browse internship listings and role descriptions, compare herself negatively to stronger candidates, save side-project ideas she never scopes properly, and work alone longer than she should. She defaults back to watching tutorials or prompting AI assistants to "feel productive". While she is highly comfortable with modular technical tools, she is significantly less comfortable with independent project scoping, role definition, tracing bugs in synthesized code, and cross-functional collaboration.

**Tech comfort**  
* **Very High**: Comfortable with Git syntax, IDEs, command line tools, cloud basics, prompting for localized snippets, and learning from documentation. 
* **Friction Points**: Much less comfortable with ambiguous product requirements, multi-file software architecture, and agile project management frameworks.

**Preferred channels**  
Handshake and employer career pages for internships, LinkedIn for role research, GitHub for social proof, Discord/group chats for peers, and university career center resources when deadlines get real. This channel mix is consistent with how official career resources position job searching, employer discovery, and short-form professional project experiences.

**What she needs from the platform**  
She needs a structured bridge out of tutorial and AI hell. This includes pre-scoped project tracks, a defined developer role, and teammates who are serious but still beginner-friendly. She requires weekly milestones to prevent scope creep, clear deliverable boundaries to force her to understand and document her code, and a final package she can reuse in applications: a deployed demo, a clean GitHub repo with active contribution logs, a README, a short architecture note, and optimized resume bullets. This target is reinforced by career-center guidance stating that project-based experiences and portfolios help students demonstrate concrete skills to recruiters.

**Sample resume bullets**

* Designed a Python and SQL dashboard analyzing user behavior data, identifying three retention issues and proposing product changes backed by quantified findings.

### Persona B

**Name:** Daniel Brooks  
**Age / Year:** 20, Sophomore or Junior  
**Major:** Business Economics, Information Systems, or Economics  
**Career Goal:** Business analyst, consulting, product-adjacent, strategy, or marketing internship  
**Best-fit internships and jobs:** Bain Associate Consultant Intern, McKinsey Summer Business Analyst, BCG consulting internship, Capital One Business Analytics, Strategy Consulting, Product, or Management internship, Google APMM-type early-career track. 

**Background**  
Daniel is career-motivated and often “high-agency” on paper, but his experience is too generic. He may have good grades, some club exposure, and strong interest in strategy, startups, or product, yet still lacks a concrete body of work that shows he can analyze a problem, structure a recommendation, and collaborate across functions. This is especially important in consulting and product-adjacent hiring, where firms openly emphasize communication, independent ownership, and solving complex business problems in teams. 

**Motivations and goals**  
Daniel wants experience that is legible to recruiters. He is not looking for “busywork”; he wants something he can discuss in interviews as evidence of leadership, initiative, market understanding, stakeholder reasoning, and execution. A strong project gives him exactly that: a PRD, strategy deck, market analysis, customer-insight memo, or pilot recommendation he can defend. Official employer pages support this logic: Google’s APMM program accepts experience gained through full-time roles, internships, projects, or academic classes, and Capital One’s product, strategy consulting, business analytics, and management programs all emphasize strategy, business decisions, customer needs, and cross-functional initiative.

**Pain points**  
Daniel’s biggest problem is ambiguity. He does not know which experiences matter most for product versus consulting versus marketing versus analyst roles. He often senses that “PM” sounds attractive, but official role structures suggest that many pure PM roles require prior experience, while undergraduate-friendly entry points are more often business analyst, strategy, consulting, product-adjacent, or product marketing pathways. That means he needs guidance on what to build now, not just inspiration. This is an inference from Google PM postings, Adobe’s MBA PM internship, Google’s APMM program, and undergraduate internship pathways at consulting firms and Capital One. 

**Typical behaviors**  
Daniel reads career newsletters, watches internship advice content, attends employer sessions, and talks about startups or strategy with friends. He starts frameworks easily but can drift without deadlines. He is comfortable with slides, spreadsheets, docs, and research synthesis, but less comfortable writing code or transforming vague ideas into tangible weekly outputs.

**Tech comfort**  
Moderate to high. Very comfortable with docs, spreadsheets, slides, collaboration tools, and analytics basics. May have limited technical depth, but can work effectively with technical teammates.

**Preferred channels**  
Handshake, LinkedIn, company career pages, university consulting and business clubs, employer events, and career-center resources. That preference aligns with official university guidance on internship search, employer events, and exploratory experiences such as micro-internships and consulting pathways. 

**What he needs from the platform**  
He needs career-track-specific projects, explicit role definitions such as Product Lead, Business Analyst, Growth Lead, or Strategy Lead, deliverable templates, and a final output that looks like real work. He also needs tight project framing so that “collaboration” becomes something concrete: customer interviews, competitor analysis, prioritization, go-to-market choices, operating recommendations, and executive presentation.

#### Recommended project tracks and outputs for Daniel

| Project track | Recommended final outputs | Best internship or job mapping |
|---|---|---|
| Product discovery sprint | PRD, user interview summary, prioritization matrix, roadmap slide, resume bullets | Product internship, APMM, product operations, business analyst.|
| Consulting-style business case | Issue tree, market sizing, competitive analysis, recommendation deck, executive summary | Bain ACI, McKinsey Summer Business Analyst, BCG consulting internship.|
| Growth or go-to-market project | Campaign brief, KPI framework, channel test plan, experiment readout deck | Product marketing, growth marketing, business analytics. |
| Startup validation project | Customer interview notes, landing page test, business model one-pager, pitch deck | Startup intern, strategy intern, management intern, product-adjacent role.|
| Operations and management project | Process map, risk log, operating recommendation memo, stakeholder presentation | Management, project/program, operations, internal strategy roles.|

**Sample resume bullets**  
- Led a four-person product discovery project, conducting 12 user interviews and writing a PRD with prioritized features, KPIs, and rollout recommendations for a student-facing mobile concept.  
- Built a consulting-style recommendation deck analyzing a campus service market, synthesizing competitor research and survey data into a five-part operating plan presented to stakeholders.


### Persona C

**Name:** Maya Hernandez  
**Age / Year:** 20, Sophomore or Junior  
**Major:** Design, Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), Cognitive Science, Psychology, Communication, Computer Science, or a related field  
**Career Goal:** UI/UX Designer, Product Designer, UX Researcher, Design Systems Intern, or Product Design Intern  
**Best-fit internships and jobs:** Google UX Design Intern, Microsoft Product Design Intern, Adobe Design Internship, Figma Design Internship, UX Research Internship, Product Design Internship, Design Systems Internship, or other entry-level UX and product design pathways.

**Background** Maya is a motivated student who wants to build a career in UI/UX or product design, but her experience is mostly limited to coursework, tutorials, design challenges, and personal redesign projects. She understands design fundamentals and tools such as Figma, but she lacks portfolio-ready projects that demonstrate how she approaches real user problems from research through final solution. Like many aspiring designers, Maya knows that recruiters evaluate portfolios more heavily than grades. However, finding realistic projects and committed collaborators is difficult. Most of her work is completed independently, making it harder to demonstrate teamwork, stakeholder communication, and cross-functional collaboration. She wants opportunities that feel closer to real product work and produce tangible artifacts she can confidently discuss during interviews.

**Motivations and goals** Maya wants portfolio projects that are credible and recruiter-friendly. She is not looking for isolated design exercises or simple screen redesigns. Instead, she wants experiences that demonstrate her ability to understand user needs, conduct research, define product problems, wireframe, prototype, test, and collaborate with cross-functional teammates. A strong project gives her a complete case study that showcases both process and outcomes. The final result should help her stand out in internship applications and portfolio reviews.

**Pain points** Maya's biggest challenge is building portfolio evidence that feels realistic and professionally relevant. Most of her projects come from classes or personal practice, which often lack the depth and structure expected in internship portfolios. She faces uncertainty about what recruiters value most in a beginner portfolio—such as the proper balance between research, visual design, and storytelling. Furthermore, because she works independently, she has limited experience working with developers or product managers, and she struggles to find committed students who share similar goals.

**Typical behaviors** Maya spends time improving her skills by watching UX and product design tutorials, practicing in Figma, exploring professional portfolios online, and participating in design clubs or communities. She follows designers on LinkedIn, completes redesign challenges, and actively seeks feedback from peers. She is highly motivated but lacks a structured environment that turns learning into portfolio-ready outcomes.

**Tech comfort** Moderate to high. Very comfortable with design tools such as Figma, FigJam, Canva, and presentation software. Familiar with basic prototyping, wireframing, and design systems concepts. May have limited coding experience but can collaborate effectively with technical teammates.

**Preferred channels** LinkedIn, Handshake, design clubs, portfolio communities, UX Discord groups, university career centers, employer events, and design-focused networking opportunities.

**What she needs from the platform** She needs portfolio-focused projects, clear role expectations, structured milestones, and opportunities to collaborate with students from technical and business backgrounds. Specifically, she benefits from UX/product design tracks, templates for research and usability testing, portfolio case-study frameworks, and cross-functional team matching. Most importantly, she needs a process that transforms collaboration into tangible outputs such as research findings, wireframes, prototypes, usability reports, and portfolio-ready case studies.

#### Recommended project tracks and outputs for Maya

| Project track | Recommended final outputs | Best internship or job mapping |
|---|---|---|
| Mobile app redesign | User research summary, wireframes, high-fidelity prototype, UX case study | UX Design Intern, Product Design Intern |
| Product discovery and UX research | Interview findings, personas, journey maps, usability recommendations | UX Research Intern, Product Design Intern |
| Campus platform redesign | Information architecture, user flows, prototype, case study | Product Design, UX Design |
| Design systems project | Component library, design documentation, implementation recommendations | Design Systems Intern |
| Startup product design project | Research synthesis, prototype, usability testing report, presentation deck | Product Design, Startup Design Intern |

**Sample resume bullets** - Designed a mobile-first product experience by conducting user research, creating wireframes, building a high-fidelity Figma prototype, and incorporating usability feedback into final design recommendations.  
- Collaborated with a cross-functional team to redesign a student-facing platform, producing user flows, design systems components, and a portfolio-ready UX case study documenting the end-to-end design process.
